Understanding Neuropathic Pain

Neuropathic pain arises from damage or dysfunction within the nervous system itself ‚Äî the peripheral nerves, spinal cord, or brain ‚Äî rather than from ongoing tissue injury. This fundamental distinction from nociceptive pain explains why neuropathic pain often persists long after any apparent injury has healed, responds poorly to standard pain medications, and requires a specialized approach to achieve meaningful relief.

Patients consistently describe neuropathic pain with characteristic terms: burning, shooting, stabbing, electric shock-like, or "pins and needles." It is often accompanied by allodynia (pain from stimuli that should not hurt, like light touch or clothing contact) and hyperalgesia (exaggerated pain response to normally mildly painful stimuli). These features reflect abnormal neural processing ‚Äî which is why treatments targeting the nervous system directly, such as nerve blocks and spinal cord stimulation, often succeed where standard analgesics fail.

Common Causes of Neuropathic Pain

  • Diabetic peripheral neuropathy ‚Äî the most common cause of neuropathy in the U.S., affecting approximately 50% of people with longstanding diabetes; typically causes bilateral burning, tingling, and numbness starting in the feet
  • Cervical and lumbar radiculopathy ‚Äî nerve root compression from disc herniation or spinal stenosis producing radicular pain in the arm or leg
  • Complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS) ‚Äî a severe neuropathic condition characterized by burning pain, skin changes, swelling, and autonomic dysfunction following an injury or surgery
  • Post-herpetic neuralgia ‚Äî persistent burning pain following a shingles outbreak, caused by damage to affected peripheral nerves
  • Chemotherapy-induced peripheral neuropathy (CIPN) ‚Äî nerve damage from certain chemotherapy agents causing numbness, tingling, and pain in hands and feet
  • Small fiber neuropathy ‚Äî damage to unmyelinated C fibers producing burning pain, often with normal standard nerve conduction studies
  • Traumatic nerve injury ‚Äî direct injury to peripheral nerves from trauma, surgery, or compression

Symptoms of Neuropathic Pain

  • Burning, shooting, or electric shock-like pain in the arms, legs, or trunk
  • Tingling, numbness, or "pins and needles" that may be constant or intermittent
  • Allodynia ‚Äî pain from light touch, temperature changes, or clothing contact
  • Hyperalgesia ‚Äî exaggerated, prolonged pain response to stimuli
  • Weakness in affected limbs
  • Sleep disruption due to nocturnal pain worsening
  • The paradox of pain in areas that feel numb ‚Äî a hallmark of neuropathic conditions
  • Skin color changes, temperature differences, or abnormal sweating (especially in CRPS)

Diagnostic Evaluation

Accurate diagnosis requires identifying both the type of pain (neuropathic vs. nociceptive vs. mixed) and the underlying cause. Dr. Qureshi uses a combination of clinical history, neurological examination, electrodiagnostic studies (EMG and nerve conduction), and imaging to characterize the pain mechanism. For suspected small fiber neuropathy, skin punch biopsy measuring intraepidermal nerve fiber density provides definitive diagnosis when standard nerve conduction studies are normal.

Treatment Options

Neuropathic pain often requires a multimodal treatment strategy combining interventional procedures with medication optimization:

  • Nerve blocks ‚Äî targeted injections of local anesthetic and/or steroid adjacent to specific pain-generating nerves; provide both diagnostic confirmation and therapeutic relief lasting weeks to months
  • Spinal cord stimulation (SCS) ‚Äî the most powerful interventional tool for refractory neuropathic pain; FDA-approved for diabetic neuropathy, CRPS, and failed back surgery syndrome with strong evidence for 50%+ pain reduction in appropriate candidates
  • Epidural steroid injections ‚Äî for radicular neuropathic pain from nerve root compression
  • Sympathetic nerve blocks ‚Äî for CRPS and sympathetically mediated pain; interrupts the abnormal sympathetic contribution to pain
  • Medication optimization ‚Äî gabapentinoids, SNRIs, and topical agents as part of a comprehensive, opioid-minimizing plan

CRPS: A Specialized Focus

Complex regional pain syndrome (CRPS) deserves special mention as one of the most severe and disabling neuropathic pain conditions. It typically develops following a limb injury and is characterized by pain far out of proportion to the original injury, along with skin color and temperature changes, swelling, and abnormal sweating. Early aggressive treatment is critical: outcomes are significantly better when treatment begins within the first 3‚Äì6 months. Dr. Qureshi has specific expertise in CRPS management, including sympathetic nerve blocks and spinal cord stimulation evaluation.

Frequently Asked Questions

Is neuropathic pain permanent?
Not necessarily. When the underlying cause can be addressed ‚Äî nerve decompression for a herniated disc, blood sugar optimization for diabetic neuropathy, early aggressive treatment for CRPS ‚Äî significant improvement or full resolution is achievable. Spinal cord stimulation has transformed outcomes for conditions once considered permanently disabling, achieving 50‚Äì70% pain reduction in properly selected patients.
What is the most effective treatment for diabetic neuropathy pain?
Blood sugar optimization is the most important foundational step ‚Äî tight glucose control can slow progression. For pain management, first-line evidence-based treatments include duloxetine, pregabalin, and gabapentin. For patients whose pain is inadequately controlled with medications, spinal cord stimulation has FDA approval for painful diabetic neuropathy with strong evidence from randomized trials.
How do I know if my pain is neuropathic vs. musculoskeletal?
Neuropathic pain is typically burning, shooting, electric, or like pins-and-needles, often following a nerve distribution pattern. It may be accompanied by allodynia. Musculoskeletal pain is more often aching, stiff, or tender with palpation, related to movement and position. Many patients have mixed pain with both components, which is why accurate characterization affects treatment selection.
What is central sensitization?
Central sensitization is a state of amplified pain processing within the central nervous system where pain signals are turned up abnormally. It develops secondary to chronic pain and explains why chronic pain patients often experience pain disproportionate to imaging findings. Treatments targeting the central nervous system ‚Äî spinal cord stimulation, SNRIs, anticonvulsants ‚Äî are most effective for this component.
Does insurance cover neuropathy treatment?
Coverage depends on the specific treatment. Most insurance plans cover diagnostic evaluation and medication management for neuropathic conditions. Nerve blocks, epidural injections, and spinal cord stimulation are covered when medical necessity criteria are met. Our office handles insurance verification and prior authorization for all procedures.
