### What Is Kyphoplasty?

Kyphoplasty is a minimally invasive spinal procedure used to treat vertebral compression fractures — fractures where one or more vertebrae in the spine collapse or become wedge-shaped, causing pain, loss of height, and progressive spinal deformity.

During the procedure, Dr. Qureshi inserts a small balloon catheter into the fractured vertebra through a needle-sized incision. The balloon is carefully inflated to restore the vertebra toward its original height, creating a cavity inside the bone. The balloon is then removed and the cavity is filled with medical-grade bone cement that stabilizes the fracture and prevents further collapse.

Kyphoplasty typically takes 30–60 minutes per vertebral level treated, is performed under fluoroscopic (live X-ray) guidance for precision, and most patients go home the same day. Pain relief is often dramatic — many patients report significant improvement within 48 hours.

### Who Needs Kyphoplasty?

Vertebral compression fractures are among the most common fractures in adults over 50, affecting an estimated 750,000 Americans each year. They are most frequently caused by:

**Osteoporosis** — the leading cause of compression fractures. As bone density decreases with age, vertebrae become vulnerable to fracture from everyday activities like bending, lifting, or even coughing. Women over 50 are at highest risk, particularly after menopause.

**Spinal metastases** — cancer that has spread to the spine can weaken vertebral bone, leading to pathologic compression fractures. Kyphoplasty can provide rapid pain relief and structural stabilization in these cases.

**Trauma** — falls, car accidents, and other injuries can cause compression fractures, particularly in patients with underlying bone weakness.

You may be a candidate for kyphoplasty if you have a confirmed vertebral compression fracture and experience persistent back pain that has not improved with conservative treatment (rest, bracing, pain medication) over 2–6 weeks. Dr. Qureshi uses MRI imaging to confirm that the fracture is recent and actively causing your symptoms before recommending the procedure.

### How Kyphoplasty Is Performed

**Before the procedure:** You will have an MRI to confirm the fracture is acute (recent) and treatable with kyphoplasty. Dr. Qureshi will review your imaging, medical history, and medications. Blood thinners may need to be temporarily paused prior to the procedure.

**During the procedure:** Kyphoplasty is performed under local anesthesia with sedation — you are comfortable but do not require general anesthesia in most cases. Using continuous fluoroscopic guidance, Dr. Qureshi makes a small incision (approximately 1 cm) and advances a specialized needle into the fractured vertebra through the pedicle — a strong bony corridor that provides safe access to the vertebral body.

A balloon tamp is then inserted through the needle and gradually inflated. This serves two purposes: it restores vertebral height that was lost due to the fracture, and it compresses the surrounding cancellous bone to create a well-defined cavity. Once optimal height restoration is achieved, the balloon is deflated and removed.

The cavity is then filled with polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA) bone cement under low pressure. The cement hardens within minutes, creating an internal cast that stabilizes the fracture. The thick consistency of the cement and the contained cavity created by the balloon significantly reduce the risk of cement leakage compared to vertebroplasty.

**After the procedure:** You will be monitored for 1–2 hours before going home the same day. Most patients can walk within hours. Avoid heavy lifting for 2–4 weeks. Pain relief begins as soon as the cement stabilizes, with full benefit typically realized within 48 hours.

### Kyphoplasty vs. Vertebroplasty

Both kyphoplasty and vertebroplasty treat vertebral compression fractures with bone cement, but kyphoplasty adds the balloon inflation step. This distinction matters for three reasons:

First, the balloon restores vertebral height, which vertebroplasty cannot predictably achieve. Height restoration matters because progressive height loss leads to kyphotic deformity (forward curvature), which affects breathing, balance, and quality of life.

Second, the cavity created by the balloon allows cement to be injected under lower pressure into a contained space, reducing the risk of cement leaking into surrounding tissues — the most common complication of vertebroplasty.

Third, clinical studies show that kyphoplasty produces equivalent or superior pain relief compared to vertebroplasty with a more favorable safety profile. Dr. Qureshi will recommend the most appropriate approach based on your specific fracture pattern and clinical situation.

### Results and Recovery

Clinical data shows that kyphoplasty provides significant pain reduction in approximately 90% of patients with acute vertebral compression fractures. Most patients report that their pain improves from severe to mild or none within the first week.

Recovery is rapid compared to traditional spine surgery. Most patients return to daily activities within a few days. Physical activity is gradually increased over 2–4 weeks. Dr. Qureshi may also recommend bone density testing and osteoporosis treatment to reduce your risk of future fractures — addressing the underlying cause, not just the symptom.

## FAQ Section

**Q: Is kyphoplasty painful?**
A: The procedure is performed under local anesthesia with sedation, so you should feel minimal discomfort during the treatment. You may feel pressure when the balloon is inflated. Post-procedure soreness at the incision site is mild and typically resolves within a few days.

**Q: How long does kyphoplasty take?**
A: The procedure takes approximately 30–60 minutes per vertebral level treated. Including preparation and recovery time, expect to spend about 3–4 hours at the facility. Most patients go home the same day.

**Q: How soon will I feel better after kyphoplasty?**
A: Many patients report noticeable pain improvement within 48 hours. Some feel relief as early as the day of the procedure once the bone cement has hardened. Full benefit is typically realized within the first week.

**Q: What are the risks of kyphoplasty?**
A: Kyphoplasty is considered a safe, minimally invasive procedure. The most commonly discussed risk is cement leakage outside the vertebral body, though the balloon technique significantly reduces this compared to vertebroplasty. Other rare risks include infection, bleeding, and temporary increase in pain. Dr. Qureshi will discuss all risks and benefits during your consultation.

**Q: Does insurance cover kyphoplasty?**
A: Yes, most major insurance plans and Medicare cover kyphoplasty when performed for an indicated vertebral compression fracture. Our office will verify your coverage before the procedure. Call (281) 982-2144 to check your eligibility.
