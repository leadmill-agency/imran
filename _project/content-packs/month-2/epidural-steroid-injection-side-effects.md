## Article Content

### Introduction

Epidural steroid injections (ESIs) are one of the most widely performed procedures in pain management, with millions administered each year in the United States. They are a proven, evidence-based treatment for conditions like herniated discs, spinal stenosis, sciatica, and radiculopathy — delivering anti-inflammatory corticosteroid medication directly to the inflamed nerve roots in your spine.

If your doctor has recommended an epidural steroid injection, it is completely normal to have questions about side effects. Understanding what to expect — both the common, temporary effects and the rare complications — helps you make an informed decision and feel prepared going into your procedure.

This guide covers the full picture: what most patients actually experience, what the medical literature says about risks, and what signs should prompt you to contact your doctor afterward.

### Common Side Effects (What Most Patients Experience)

The majority of side effects from epidural steroid injections are mild, temporary, and resolve on their own within a few days. These are not complications — they are normal responses to the procedure and the medication.

**Temporary pain flare at the injection site.** This is the most commonly reported side effect. The injection itself involves a needle passing through soft tissue to reach the epidural space, and some localized soreness is expected. Additionally, the steroid medication can cause a temporary increase in pain before the anti-inflammatory effect takes hold — typically lasting 1–3 days. Applying ice for 15–20 minutes at a time helps manage this flare.

**Steroid flush.** Some patients experience facial flushing, a feeling of warmth, or mild redness in the face and chest for 1–2 days after the injection. This is a systemic response to the corticosteroid and is harmless, though it can feel unusual if you are not expecting it. It is more common in women and typically resolves without treatment.

**Temporary blood sugar elevation.** Corticosteroids temporarily increase blood glucose levels. For most patients, this is clinically insignificant and self-correcting. However, if you have diabetes, this is an important consideration — blood sugar may remain elevated for 3–7 days after the injection. Your doctor should be aware of your diabetes before the procedure so you can adjust your monitoring and medication plan accordingly.

**Mild headache.** A mild headache after an epidural injection is not uncommon, particularly with interlaminar approaches. This usually resolves within 24–48 hours with rest and hydration. A severe or positional headache (one that worsens when you stand and improves when you lie down) may indicate a dural puncture and should be reported to your doctor immediately.

**Sleep disturbance.** Corticosteroids can temporarily affect sleep patterns, causing insomnia or restless sleep for 1–3 nights following the injection. This is a known pharmacologic effect of steroids and is self-limiting.

**Water retention and mild weight gain.** Corticosteroids can cause temporary fluid retention, leading to mild bloating or a small increase in weight. This resolves as the steroid is metabolized, usually within 1–2 weeks.

### Less Common Side Effects

These effects occur in a smaller percentage of patients and are still generally self-resolving.

**Temporary leg weakness or numbness.** If the local anesthetic used during the procedure spreads to nearby nerve roots, you may experience temporary numbness, tingling, or weakness in one or both legs. This typically lasts 1–4 hours and is the reason you are advised not to drive yourself home after the procedure.

**Anxiety or mood changes.** Corticosteroids can temporarily affect mood, causing feelings of anxiety, restlessness, irritability, or even brief euphoria. These effects are dose-dependent and typically subside within a few days.

**Decreased immune function.** Corticosteroids suppress immune activity — that is part of how they reduce inflammation. There is a brief period of mildly reduced immune function after the injection. This is rarely clinically significant for a single injection, but it is one reason doctors typically limit the number of ESIs to 3–4 per year.

### Rare but Serious Side Effects

Serious complications from epidural steroid injections are uncommon when the procedure is performed by a trained interventional specialist using fluoroscopic guidance. However, as with any medical procedure, they do exist and should be understood.

**Infection.** Epidural abscess or infection is a rare but serious complication. The risk is minimized through strict sterile technique. Signs to watch for include increasing pain several days after the injection, fever, chills, or redness and swelling at the injection site. Contact your doctor immediately if you develop these symptoms.

**Dural puncture ("wet tap").** If the needle inadvertently penetrates the dural membrane surrounding the spinal cord, spinal fluid may leak out. This can cause a post-dural puncture headache — a distinctive, severe headache that worsens when upright and improves when lying flat. This occurs in approximately 1–5% of interlaminar epidural injections and is treatable with rest, hydration, caffeine, and in persistent cases, a blood patch procedure.

**Nerve damage.** Direct nerve injury is extremely rare, particularly when fluoroscopic guidance is used to confirm needle placement. Transient nerve irritation — a brief shooting pain during the injection — may occur and typically resolves immediately.

**Epidural hematoma.** Bleeding in the epidural space is a very rare complication, primarily a concern in patients taking blood thinners. This is why anticoagulant medications are typically held before the procedure according to established safety guidelines.

**Allergic reaction.** Allergic reactions to the contrast dye, local anesthetic, or steroid medication can occur but are rare. Inform your doctor of any known drug allergies before the procedure.

### How to Minimize Side Effects

Several steps can reduce your risk of side effects and improve your overall experience.

**Choose an experienced, fellowship-trained interventional pain specialist.** Fluoroscopy-guided injections performed by a trained specialist have the lowest complication rates. The precision of needle placement directly affects both the safety and effectiveness of the injection.

**Disclose your complete medical history.** This includes diabetes, blood thinners, bleeding disorders, active infections, allergies, and pregnancy. These factors affect how the procedure is planned and performed.

**Follow pre- and post-procedure instructions.** Your doctor will provide specific guidance on medications to hold, activity restrictions, and what to monitor after the injection. Following these instructions significantly reduces risk.

**Do not exceed recommended frequency.** Current guidelines generally recommend no more than 3–4 epidural steroid injections per year to minimize the cumulative effects of corticosteroids on bone density, blood sugar, and immune function.

### When to Call Your Doctor

Contact your doctor or seek medical attention if you experience any of the following after an epidural steroid injection:

- Fever above 101°F
- Severe headache that worsens when standing
- Progressive weakness or numbness in your legs
- Loss of bladder or bowel control
- Increasing pain that worsens rather than improves over several days
- Redness, swelling, or drainage at the injection site

These symptoms are uncommon but warrant prompt evaluation.

### The Bottom Line

Epidural steroid injections have a well-established safety profile supported by decades of clinical use. The vast majority of patients experience only mild, temporary side effects that resolve within a few days. Serious complications are rare, particularly when the procedure is performed by an experienced specialist using image guidance.

The decision to proceed with an ESI should weigh the known side effects against the potential benefit — significant pain relief that can improve your function, reduce your need for oral medications, and help you avoid or delay surgery.

## FAQ Section

**Q: How long do epidural steroid injection side effects last?**
A: Most common side effects — injection site soreness, steroid flush, sleep disturbance — resolve within 1–5 days. Blood sugar elevation in diabetic patients may last 3–7 days. The therapeutic anti-inflammatory effect typically develops over 3–7 days and can last weeks to months.

**Q: Can epidural steroid injections cause weight gain?**
A: Temporary water retention and mild weight gain can occur due to the corticosteroid. This is typically 1–3 pounds and resolves within 1–2 weeks. Long-term weight gain is not expected from occasional epidural injections.

**Q: Are epidural steroid injections safe for diabetics?**
A: Yes, but with additional precautions. Corticosteroids temporarily elevate blood sugar, so diabetic patients should monitor glucose more frequently for 5–7 days after the injection and may need to adjust insulin or medication. Inform Dr. Qureshi about your diabetes during your consultation.

**Q: How many epidural steroid injections can I have per year?**
A: Current guidelines generally recommend no more than 3–4 per year to minimize cumulative steroid effects. The exact number depends on your response to treatment and individual risk factors. Dr. Qureshi will discuss the appropriate frequency for your situation.
